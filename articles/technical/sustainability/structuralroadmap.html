The Structural Engineering Roadmap

How I would learn Structural Engineering if I could start all over

By Chukwuma Chibuike

Are you a civil/structural engineering student, at the undergraduate or graduate level? Or a young engineering professional with the need to reinvent yourself as a structural engineer? Or do you know someone who might need this?

Here is your surefire way to learn structural engineering thoroughly and land a real job in no time.

By knowing the correct and exact things to focus on if you want to be a structural engineer, you avoid the unnecessary pitfall of wasting your time doing things that don't work and that will not add much value to your future career as a structural engineer.

So, in this article, you will learn what skills you need to focus on because it is what you will use in real-life engineering practice.

Categorize your learning path for structural engineering

Let us categorize your learning path into five basic sections, namely:

  1. Preliminary Knowledge
  2. Theoretical Basis of Structural Engineering
  3. Software for Structural Engineering
  4. Practical Knowledge
  5. Experience

You should use these categories as a guide to direct your efforts and monitor your progress.

1. Preliminary Knowledge

Many students or even graduate engineers in the field try to learn or practice structural engineering without having a solid basis in mathematics.

This is a mistake which makes a lot of structural engineering concepts unnecessarily difficult to understand, let alone master.

I remember many of my fellow students during my undergraduate years complaining about how difficult structural engineering was. But I knew what their problem was. They did not know enough mathematics to succeed as engineering students.

You must realize that engineering is simply the application of scientific knowledge and principles. And structural engineering is basically an application of mathematics and physics. So without proper grounding in mathematics, it will be an uphill battle for you.

So, do not make the same mistake of allowing your fundamentals in mathematics shaky.

The mathematical concepts you need to know is not that difficult to grasp. You were probably just afraid for no reason, didn't like your math teacher, or had a bad math teacher.

Whichever one it was, no problems.

If you're weak in Math, the links below will direct you to YouTube videos and short playlists that will help you transition from someone who finds mathematical concepts difficult to someone who can use them proficiently to solve engineering problems.

Go here to relearn College Algebra quickly.

Then try to understand Linear Algebra using this crash course on linear algebra.

Differential Equations is necessary but optional if you are in a haste. But make sure to learn it if you want to go as far as learning how to use Finite Element Analysis in structural engineering.

Note: Do not misunderstand the issue of mathematics here. You can totally binge watch these mathematics playlists just to understand the concepts. I'm not trying to turn you into a math wizard.

After all you'll most likely never solve difficult mathematical problems by hand while on the job. There are numerous structural engineering software that do all the tough computations, and every engineering firm uses them.

You simply need to understand what these software packages do and how they do it to use them proficiently.

You will only need to be a math expert if you're part of a team that creates some of the commercial structural analysis and design software.

So for most jobs, a simple understanding of these mathematical principles will surely make everything you get to study and do in structural engineering easy.

2. Theoretical Basis of Structural Engineering

Statics

You need to understand the first principles of structural mechanics. This means you need a solid foundation on engineering statics for a start.

In engineering statics, you will learn how to apply the knowledge of vectors, which you learned from physics and linear algebra, to engineering problems. You will master both 2D and 3D vector applications for forces and displacements.

You will then master how to derive the centroids and moments of inertia for different shapes.

You will take your knowledge further by mastering how to resolve the forces and moments acting on beams, columns, and frames using the three equations of static equilibrium.

Professor Gregory Michaelson of the department of civil engineering in Marshal University is generous enough to give you a hand. Learn Engineering Statics here.

Then solidify your knowledge of statics here by solving problems.

Mechanics of Deformable Materials

You will take your knowledge of statics further by mastering the mechanics of engineering materials. Here you will deepen your understanding of what support reactions and internal responses of structural systems are.

You will also learn how to analyze the internal responses, stresses, and strains in statically indeterminate structures.

You will gain a solid grounding in the examination of the stresses and strains that develop in loaded structural systems due to these internal responses and then learn how to calculate the slopes and deflections of members such as beams and frames.

You will learn how to solve for the slopes and displacements of structural members using the method of superposition, virtual work, and Castigliano's theorem.

Furthermore, you will learn the concept of column buckling under axial compression.

Study Mechanics of Deformable Bodies here.

And solidify your knowledge Mechanics of Materials through problem solving.

Structural Analysis

You will take your knowledge of engineering mechanics further by mastering how to analyze structural members using energy methods.

You will study load take down for multi-storey structures using tributary areas and how to draw influence lines for moving loads over structures such as roofs, slabs and bridges.

You will master the use of energy methods such as the Virtual Work Principle and Castigliano’s theorem. And learn techniques such as the 3-moment equation, the force method, moment area method, and slope deflection method.

You will solidify and perfect your skills for drawing and interpreting shear force and bending moment diagrams and analyzing indeterminate structures.

At this stage, you must already start trying your hands on using software to aid you in structural analysis. We will cover the software you will use in the “Software” section.

Learn Structural Analysis here with Professor Gregory Michaelson.

And deepen your knowledge through problem solving in structural analysis.

Civil Engineering Materials

Though the studies on the Mechanics of Deformable Materials gave you the skills you need to understand how engineering materials perform under loading and in use as structural members, this course is civil engineering specific. It will teach you useful skills you need in your repertoire as a structural engineer.

You will learn more about concrete and the individual materials used for concrete.

You will study aggregates and their classifications ---

  • fine aggregates or sand, and
  • coarse aggregates such as stones, and chippings.

You will learn about their engineering behavior when used in concrete and how to perform laboratory testing to ascertain the properties of these materials.

You will understand material testing and grading experiments such as sieve analysis, tensile and compressive strength tests, and how to compute stresses and strains for these materials.

You will also gain understanding about asphalt.

This course explains how to mix fine and coarse aggregates and water with Portland cement or bitumen to produce cement concrete or asphalt concrete.

Finally, you will learn about the engineering characteristics of steel and timber prior to learning how to design them as structural members.

Learn about Civil Engineering Materials here.

Structural Steel Design

Steel is one of the most important structural materials. It is ubiquitous and can be used for almost any structural application both within and beyond civil engineering.

It is cheap and durable and easy to mold into any shape necessary for the application it is to be used for.

This is why a complete course is dedicated for the design of structural steel. There are structural engineering experts completely specialized in the design and construction of steel structures.

So you must take your study of steel analysis, design, and applications very seriously.

Firstly you must master the use of steel codes and manuals of the country where you live, such as the American Institute of Steel Construction or the British Standard.

You must also understand the material behavior of steel and the various steel sections and sizes used regularly in structural engineering. They are all described in depth in the steel manuals and code.

Examples of some sections include the W-beams, I-beams, Channel beams, rectangular and circular sections, etc.

You will learn the different weight to length ratios of the different sections, how, why, where, and when to choose a particular section to use for a particular design, bearing in mind the serviceability and strength limit states you need to achieve in your design.

You must also endeavour to know how to use software to speed up your design of steel structures such as beams, and trusses.

Learn the Design of Structural Steel here.

Reinforced Concrete Design

Concrete and steel are two of the most used structural materials in structural engineering.

There is hardly any structural engineer who will not know how to analyze and design concrete structures.

So you must dedicate a lot of energy and time to master the design of concrete members such as beams, columns, slabs, and staircases. You must also learn how to analyze and design foundation members such as pads and footings.

You must understand the material behavior of concrete and how to mix and produce concrete of any desired strength, bearing in mind the mix ratio of the Portland cement, fine and coarse aggregates, and necessary moisture content.

You must understand the different methods of concrete production such as in situ mixing of concrete on the site of use, precast concrete, and pre-stressed concrete.

You must understand concrete curing, testing, the compressive and tensile behavior of concrete, why steel bar reinforcements and stirrups are used to reinforce concrete to improve its performance in tension, and the capacity of concrete to withstand high shear forces, tensile forces, and bending.

You must master how to use the design codes and manuals of your country and learn how to design to specification.

You must understand how concrete fails, such as through creep, buckling, cracking, fatigue, etc. And how to correct and repair failing concrete members in buildings.

Structural design is basically done after the analysis of the structure. The structural analysis provides us with the support reactions, internal responses, and deformations that result in each structural member due to applied loads and moments.

Based on serviceability limit states which ensures the member does not deflect too much under loading, and the strength limit states which ensures that members can withstand the internal responses without failing, we design the members to work properly without any problems.

So you must understand how to design concrete members and how to apply factors of safety to ensure that you select sections that ensure that the service loads and internal stresses are way less that the structural capacity of each member.

But you must also factor in cost considerations in your design so that you can produce safe designs at the lowest possible financial costs.

Learn Reinforced Concrete Design with Professor Gregory Michaelson here.

Geotechnical engineering/soil mechanics

As a structural engineer, you need some fundamental understanding of foundation properties. So you must try to study the calculation of allowable bearing pressure for soils, the different soil types and properties, and lateral earth and hydrostatic pressure calculations for retaining wall designs.

For the allowable bearing pressure, pay close attention to the ultimate bearing capacity of soils and net bearing pressure.

You need basic understanding of foundation and geotechnical engineering because in most projects, you as the structural engineer are still responsible for the design of foundation members. And you will also use the test results and recommendations of the geotechnical engineers to make your designs.

So you will need to understand the information, results, and recommendations they give you to be able to make proper use of them.

Learn Soil Mechanics and Foundation Engineering Design here.

As a structural engineer, you should not spend too much time on this part. Specialized geotechnical engineers have more to worry about these topics.

I only put them here so that you can maybe binge watch these two playlists to gain understanding of the basic knowledge you should have to help you work better with geotechnical engineers.

Summary of theoretical concepts you need to master in structural engineering

In summary, make sure you're well grounded in these theoretical concepts of structural engineering:

  1. Free body diagrams.
  2. Bending moment diagrams and shear force diagrams.
  3. Calculation of material properties such as center of gravity and second moment of area (moment of inertia).
  4. Solving trusses using method of joints and method of sections.
  5. Calculation of material properties such of modulus of elasticity and section modulus.
  6. Beam deflection and factors that influence it and the flexure formula for bending in beams.
  7. Load takedowns for calculating the total load acting on each individual member of a structural system. The different types of loads, their meaning and applications. Examples include wind loads, live loads, dead loads, earthquake loads, snow loads. Tributary area for each member so you can really perform load take down properly.
  8. Design of common steel members like rafters, beams, columns, struts, and bracing. The design of cold formed steel like purlins and girders.
  9. Design of reinforced concrete beams, slabs, columns, and walls. Design of prestressed concrete.
  10. Some basics of soil mechanics and foundation engineering.

3. Software for Structural Engineering

AutoCAD for drawings and detailings

It will be difficult to come across any real structural engineer who does not use AutoCAD for cad drawings in one form or the other.

This ubiquitous software is the trademark of Autodesk software company and serves almost all purposes for the drafting of buildings and production of 2D plans to be used in construction sites by contractors and for presentation to clients.

Any new engineering student must start from the get go to learn and practice how to draw buildings and site plans using AutoCAD. And you must continuously practice and develop your skills in AutoCAD.

Structural engineers are not architects though, so you're not required to master the artistic and aesthetic design of buildings.

But in the least, you're expected to know how to export design specifications from structural analysis and design software such as ETABS, SAP, Tekla etc.

And how you can import them into AutoCAD to produce your finished structural detailing and site specification drawings which are to be used on site by the construction managers and contractors.

Structural Analysis and Design Software

We already discussed about the fact that you must know how to use software packages for structural analysis and design. And this is not an opt-in situation for any structural engineer - You must use structural analysis and design software on the job.

If you work in a small sized firm that does mostly small residential constructions, many times, the construction projects are simple enough that all members necessary for the buildings have been used so many times, everywhere.

So engineers already know all the material behaviour and responses for a 12 feet by 12 feet room inside out.

Sometimes, in cases like these, no structural design is even done. They already know all the members to use and how to make and install them.

But beyond these kinds of simple projects, most engineering projects are very complicated and will take ages to be analysed and designed by hand calculations.

Some are even so complicated that hand calculations are nearly impossible.

So every structural engineer most know how to analyze and design structural members using various software.

You must learn how to use software such as ETABS, Prota, Tekla, Ansys, Abaqus, etc.

Like we discussed before, it is important that you have the mathematical grounding and the thorough understanding of structural engineering concepts to understand what these software are doing.

A car is useless to someone who cannot drive. Furthermore, if your phone crashes on the ground, burns, pr falls into liquid and refuses to turn on, a phone technician who cannot diagnose your phone's issues is useless to you. Right?

So is it with you as a structural engineer and these analysis and design software.

If you don't know or understand what you want to analyze or design, no matter how powerful Ansys, Abaqus, Catia, or Prota may be, they will be useless to you and you will be useless with them.

You must have a solid theoretical basis in structural engineering. Then you can go ahead to seek mastery in one or two of these software. You cannot learn all. Just master how to use one or two and you will be able to switch from one to the other with little effort.

Most of these software have a simple workflow with intuitive interfaces and good documentations.

Once you learn how to create the geometry and shapes of the structural members you want to design, then you will learn how to add section and material properties, and how add support conditions and specifications such as moment releases (hinges).

You will then learn how to add different types of loading conditions that suit your analysis or design purposes.

You must then know how to generate and export your reports from the analysis and design results.

And viola, you've learned how to use these kinds of software. And you can easily switch between different software packages depending on what your firm needs.

So basically the needs and preferences of your firm and colleagues will mostly determine the software you get to use as each software has its strengths and weaknesses.

ETABS for instance is very good for the design of reinforced concrete, but some other software such as Tekla may be better with steel, while SAP may be stronger for large structures such as dams.

So once you get a hang of structural analysis and design, ensure you start trying your hands with the different available software. But ensure to master at least one by learning how to use it from start to finish for complete structural analysis.

From creating the geometry to reporting and exporting results. This will serve you well in the long run.

Depending on your region, you can also check out the following software packages: Space Gass, STRIAN, RAM Concept, Skyciv, RAPT, MASTAN2, and Revit.

You cannot learn all of them because there are so many. So, just look to learn and master the few industry standards in your region.

And when you get a job, as long as you're already capable with some software packages, you can quickly pick up the exact software stack used by your firm.

CAD is CAD regardless of the software you are using.

Finite Element Analysis

Finite element analysis is not a software but a technique. But I decided to include it under this section for two reasons—

  • it is not a core requirement that you must master finite element analysis to work as a structural engineer, so I didn't want to include it in the core theoretical section
  • and you you sure cannot perform any real FEA on any structure without using software.

So this should be part of the last skills you acquire as a structural engineer as it is not compulsory for getting your feet into the job market.

But you would want to learn and master FEA if you want to be a structural engineer of reckoning and are not satisfied with remaining an average engineer.

Almost all the powerful structural analysis and design software perform Finite Element Analysis under the hood. Mastering this skill helps you understand what they're doing, their strengths and weaknesses.

Finite element analysis as a skill will serve you even beyond structural engineering.

The finite element method is roughly based on the spring equation that F = kx.

where

F represents the external force system acting on the structure, k is the spring constant, and x represents the displacement

In the case of Finite Element Analysis, the FEA software computes the Force vectors, and constructs the stiffness matrix. To solve for the displacements, the inverse of the stiffness matrix is used to multiply the force vectors to obtain the displacement vector.

Using the values of displacements, the internal forces and responses in each member of the structure, including moments, stresses, and strains are also computed.

The finite element method helps engineers move away from the struggle to solve for unknown forces in indeterminate structures. You rather solve for displacements using the stiffness matrix which is easy for structural engineering analysis and design software to compute.

To really gain thorough understanding of finite element methods, you need to understand differential equations, so your knowledge of mathematics comes in handy again here.

Mastering the application of finite element methods for solving complicated problems in structural engineering will really put you ahead of most engineers.

4. Practical Knowledge

Structural drawings

All the calculations, analysis, and design we perform as engineers must and can only be presented to the contractors and clients through structural drawings.

Without these drawings, the builders will not understand any work you perform as the structural engineer. So structural drawings are the manuals that guide and direct every work done on construction sites.

You must learn how to read structural drawings prepared by other engineers and how to prepare your own. It is absolutely impossible to work in the field of structural engineering without these skills.

Software such as AutoCAD and Revit are your go to software for preparing structural drawings.

Construction terminology

You should learn the names used to refer to construction materials and activities.

Understand and commit many building components to your memory.

In steel construction for example, you should try to know what the following components refer to: rafters, struts, bracings, flats, outriggers, purlins, plates, fin-plates, cap-plates, stiffeners, T-cleats, fillet welds, bolt sizes, butt welds, etc.

As a new graduate engineer in the field, it can be overwhelming to really learn all these different terminologies for the first time. And it's easy to get confused when some construction terminologies which you do not understand are being used constantly by your colleagues.

But to catch up quickly, research annotated diagrams for steel, timber, and concrete construction in google. Then try to commit as much as you can to your memory.

Also visit construction sites and try to identify as many components as you can. Gradually, it will become second nature to.

5. Experience

Internships

Apply for internships in small companies and consolidate what you're learning through practical application.

Even if you're still in your first year at the university, internships will be incredibly beneficial to you as it will speed up your process of learning and maturity as a future structural engineer.

And please, always choose internships in small firms over larger firms. The larger firms are big names of course and command respect. But structural engineering is a skill intensive profession. No one cares where you got your experiences as long as you have sufficient skills to do the job.

In small firms, you'll receive higher attention, and superior mentorship from the senior and resident engineers than in the bigger firms. You might also be given more challenging and hands on tasks in smaller companies. So I advise that you prioritize learning over attaching weighty names to your resume.

At this stage of your career, skills are more important.

Personal projects

To consolidate your knowledge, try to design different types of structures. Perform every engineering calculation process step by step, and produce a real, working, safe, and cost effective design with usable and industry standard structural drawings and specifications.

This will really help you improve quickly as a structural engineer. Producing many of these personal projects will make a hugely positive impact on your skill sets, giving you superior quality, proficiency, and self-reliance as an engineer.

It will also help you during job applications because you can compile them into your personal structural engineer portfolio and present them when applying for jobs.

As an example, for steel design, you can sketch up a little warehouse and pick up a virtual construction site for it in your local industrial area.

If possible, you can get the soil specifications from the government agency or labs in the area. Since you're doing it study purposes, they may be willing to give you some of these values for free.

You can then go ahead and carry out the entire design process and generate every single calculation and structural drawing necessary to build such a warehouse.

Make sure to perform all the steps accurately as if it were a real project.

Research everything you need to know along the way as you work on the project. And when you need help, reach out to professionals or university professors.

If can take your time and do many of these projects well, you might find out that you learn a lot more doing them than in all your undergraduate and graduate classes combined.
